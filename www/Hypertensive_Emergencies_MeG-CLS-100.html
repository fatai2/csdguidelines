Hypertensive Emergencies

Executive summary

Introduction

Hypertensive emergencies encompass a spectrum of clinical presentations in which uncontrolled blood pressures (BPs) lead to progressive or impending end-organ dysfunction. Blood-pressure elevations above 180/110 mm Hg can result in acute injury to the heart, brain, and the microvasculature.

If acute hypertension-mediated target-organ damage is present alongside BP elevation of > 180/110, the condition is labelled hypertensive emergency and demands immediate and aggressive treatment to limit progressive injury.

Acutely elevated blood pressure in the absence of acute target-organ damage is known as hypertensive urgency and this condition is two to three times more common than hypertensive emergency.

Neurologic end-organ damage due to uncontrolled BP may include hypertensive encephalopathy, cerebral vascular accident/cerebral infarction, subarachnoid hemorrhage, and/or intracranial hemorrhage. Cardiovascular end-organ damage may include myocardial ischemia/infarction, acute left ventricular dysfunction, acute pulmonary edema, and/or aortic dissection. Other organ systems may also be affected by uncontrolled hypertension, which may lead to acute renal failure/insufficiency, retinopathy, eclampsia, or microangiopathic hemolytic anemia.

This addresses the recognition, symptomatology and approach to the management of acute blood pressure elevations – focusing primarily on adults.

Limitations

Efficient ICU and HDU services are largely unavailable within the Gambia. The available range of parenteral antihypertensive medication is limited to labetalol and hydralazine. Troponin testing is also unavailable for routine care.


Clinical presentations of hypertensive emergencies

The most common clinical presentations of hypertensive emergencies are

  1. Cerebral infarction

  2. Pulmonary oedema

  3. Hypertensive encephalopathy

  4. Congestive heart failure

  5. Other clinical presentations associated with hypertensive emergencies include

    • Intracranial haemorrhage,

    • Aortic dissection

    • Eclampsia

    • Acute myocardial infarction

    • Retinopathy

    • Nephropathy.

History

The history and the physical examination helps to determine the nature, severity, and management of the hypertensive event. The history should focus on the presence of end-organ dysfunction, the circumstances surrounding the hypertension, and any identifiable aetiology.

Please note that patients without target organ damage are usually asymptomatic.

Important parts of the history are:

  • History of acute event: Which event (as listed above), When?, Likely cause? Course? Progression? Precipitating factors? Immediate actions taken? Any previous events-type(s),number & treatments given

  • Chest pain – think myocardial ischaemia/infarction or aortic dissection

  • Sudden upper back pain – Aortic dissection

  • Dyspnoea, Orthopnoea, PND, Palpitations – Pulmonary oedema/Congestive heart failure

  • Seizure/Visual disturbance/altered levels of consciousness – Hypertensive encephalopathy

  • Focal motor or sensory deficits and speech or visual changes – Stroke

  • The duration and severity of the patient’s pre-existing hypertension (including the degree of BP control)

  • Any symptom attributable to high blood pressure? Persistent headaches? Unexplained tinnitus? Epistaxis? Dyspnoea? Dizziness? Light-headedness? Atypical chest pain?

  • Prior investigations into the cause of the hypertension (if secondary)

  • Details of antihypertensive drug therapy and compliance,

  • Intake of over-the-counter (OTC) preparations such as sympathomimetic agents, and

  • Use of illicit drugs such as cocaine are important elements of the medication history.

  • Presence of previous end-organ dysfunction, particularly renal and cerebrovascular disease

  • Presence of any other medical problems (e.g., thyroid disease, Cushing disease, systemic lupus).

In female patients, determine the date of their last menstrual period and take a complete history of pregnancy.

History – Determination of Precipitating Factors

Addressing this has been found to contribute significantly to reducing mortality from acute severe hypertension.

Documented precipitating factors are:

  • Non-adherence to prescribed antihypertensive medications is the most common precipitating factor.

  • Dietary sodium indiscretion;

  • Use of prescribed, over-the-counter, or illicit drugs (e.g., cocaine, amphetamines, sympathomimetic agents, nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs, and high-dose glucocorticoids)

  • Anxiety or panic

  • Acute stroke

  • Heart failure

  • Acute glomerulonephritis

  • Pheochromocytoma

  • For patients on hospital admission, withholding of anti-hypertensive medications, pain, and urinary retention

Physical Examination findings

The physical examination should assess whether end-organ dysfunction is present.

  • Blood pressure measurement: BP should not only be measured in both the supine position and the standing position (assess volume depletion), but it should also be measured in both arms (a significant difference may suggest aortic dissection). BP> 180/110-120 reproducible on multiple measurements using an accurate instrument is important to ascertain the diagnosis. This must persist in spite of rest of 20-30 minutes between measurements.

  • General examination: Appearance, level of consciousness, asterixis,

  • End Organ Damage: Specific Examination

  • Fundoscopy: The presence of new retinal haemorrhages, exudates, or papilledema suggests a hypertensive emergency. Basically searching for evidence of microvascular injury or cerebral oedema (papilledema).

  • Cardiovascular system (CVS) exam: Evaluate for the presence of heart failure, which may be indicated by raised jugular venous distention, third heart sound and crackles/bibasal rales on auscultation ; peripheral oedema.

  • Central nervous system (CNS) exam: findings may include changes in the patient's level of consciousness and visual fields, and/or the presence of focal neurologic signs like motor deficits and gait, speech, and visual abnormalities

  • Abdominal Examination: Abdominal masses or bruits may be noted (Aortic dissection)

Investigations

  • ECG – extent of cardiac damage

  • Chest X-ray – congestive cardiac failure

  • Echo – extent of cardiac damage, aortic dissection

  • Electrolytes, urea and creatinine – serum creatinine level to rule out acute kidney injury

  • Full Blood Count – anaemia and thrombocytopaenia suggestive of microangiopathy

  • Urinalysis – proteinuria or haematuria points to microvascular injury

  • CT Scan – cerebrovascular accident

  • Abdominal ultrasound scan, with doppler flow – aortic dissection

  • CT of the chest and abdomen – if dissection of the aorta is not clearly shown on USS for any reason.

In the absence of symptoms, the yield of diagnostic tests are insignificant.

Treatment

Immediate Treatment

Optimal pharmacotherapy is dependent upon the specific organ at risk. In patients presenting with hypertensive emergencies, antihypertensive drug therapy has been shown to be effective in acutely decreasing BP. The details of which drug is recommended for which condition and the way to dose the drugs which are available to us are outlined in the tables on the next pages.

  • Ensure patient’s airway is patent.

  • Ascertain he/she is breathing adequately. Augment with intra-nasal oxygen if need be.

  • Make sure there are two patent intravenous cannulas through which medications and fluids will be administered.

  • Admit the patient to the open ward bed directly across the nurse sitting area.

  • For adults with a compelling condition (i.e. aortic dissection, severe preeclampsia or eclampsia, or pheochromocytoma crisis), lower SBP to below 140 mmHg during the first hour and to below 120 mmHg in aortic dissection.

  • For adults without a compelling condition, reduce the SBP by a maximum of 25% within the first hour; then, if the patient is clinically stable, lower the BP to 160/100 -110 mmHg over the next 2-6 hours, and then cautiously to normal over the following 24-48 hours.

  • In hypertensive encephalopathy, aim to reduce the MAP over 8 hours. 

  • In acute ischaemic stroke, withhold antihypertensive medications unless the SBP is above 220 mmHg or the DBP is over 120 mmHg.

  • For adults with aortic dissection, rapidly lower the SBP to below 120 mmHg within 20-30 minutes.

  • In phaeochromocytoma, ideally lower the SBP to below 140 mmHg during the first hour, with phentolamine IV bolus dose of 5 mg. Additional bolus doses may be given every 10 minutes as needed to achieve target BP. However, phentolamine is not readily available; alternative means of alpha blockade include doxazosin and magnesium sulphate. Beta blockers can be added for BP control only after alpha blockade.

  • In acute intracerebral haemorrhage with signs of increased ICP, maintain the MAP just below 130 mmHg (or SBP < 180 mmHg) for the first 24 hours after onset. In patients without increased ICP, maintain the MAP below 110 mmHg (or SBP < 160 mmHg) for the first 24 hours after symptom onset.

  • In adults with acute intracerebral haemorrhage who present with an SBP above 220 mmHg, continuous IV drug and close BP monitoring is reasonable to lower SBP. Note that it may be harmful to immediately lower SBP to below 140 mmHg in adults with spontaneous intracerebral haemorrhage who present within 6 hours of the acute event and have an SBP between 150 and 220 mmHg.

  • In women with eclampsia or preeclampsia, the SBP should lowered to below 140 mmHg during the first hour.

Delayed Treatment

This consists of lifestyle modifications.

It is recommended that to lower BP and decrease cardiovascular disease risk, 2 or more lifestyle modifications should be combined to the pharmacologic treatment:

  • Weight loss (range of approximate systolic BP reduction [SBP], 5-20 mm Hg per 10 kg)

  • Limit alcohol intake to no more than 1 oz (30 mL) of ethanol per day for men or 0.5 oz (15 mL) of ethanol per day for women and people of lighter weight (range of approximate SBP reduction, 2-4 mm Hg)

  • Reduce sodium intake to no more than 100 mmol/day (2.4 g sodium or 6 g sodium chloride; range of approximate SBP reduction, 2-8 mm Hg) 

  • Maintain adequate intake of dietary potassium (approximately 90 mmol/day)

  • Maintain adequate intake of dietary calcium and magnesium for general health

  • Stop smoking and reduce intake of dietary saturated fat and cholesterol for overall cardiovascular health

  • Engage in aerobic exercise at least 30 minutes daily for most days (range of approximate SBP reduction, 4-9 mm Hg)

Key Issues for Nursing care

  • Counselling on the importance of medication adherence-medicines do not work in people who do not take them!

  • Encourage patients to carefully consider imbibing the lifestyle measures that are documented above

  • Teach patients how to use their self-monitoring BP machines (where applicable).
