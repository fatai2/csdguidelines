Infant feeding guideline

Executive summary.

Introduction

The World Health Organization (WHO) states that ‘breastfeeding is an unequalled way of providing ideal food for the healthy growth and development of infants’. Breastfeeding is beneficial to infants, mothers, families and society, and is viewed as the biological and social norm for infant and young child feeding. Exclusive breastfeeding ensures that the infant receives the full nutritional and other advantages of breast milk, including developmental benefits and protection against infection and some chronic diseases. Breastfeeding stimulates cognitive development and protects infants against diarrhoeal infections and pneumonia. It is recommended that infants are exclusively breastfed until around 6 months of age when solid foods are introduced, and that breastfeeding is continued until 12 months of age and beyond, for as long as the mother and child desire. All staff should be trained in infant feeding so that they can enable women and their families to make informed choices about infant feeding. They should provide ongoing support in order for parents to safely and responsibly feed and care for their babies.

Key areas of focus / New additions / Changes

This guideline provides the basis for the implementation and maintenance of best practice standards for breastfeeding, based on UNICEF Baby Friendly Hospital Initiative. It acknowledges the important and clear health benefits that breastfeeding offers for both mother and babies. The Infant Feeding Guidelines are written to assist health workers provide consistent advice about breastfeeding and infant feeding. The purpose of this guideline is to support optimum infant nutrition and provide clear guidance on infant feeding. Although this guideline is relevant to healthy term infants of normal birth weight >2,500g, many of the principles of infant feeding described here can be applied to low birth weight infants (LBW).

Limitations

There are very few contraindications for breastfeeding. While exclusive breastfeeding is ideal, any amount of breast milk is beneficial to the infant and mother.

Examination findings

  • Weight: With the passage of meconium and loss of water by evaporation, an initial weight loss of up to 10% of the birth weight during the first week is normal. and by 2 weeks should have returned to his or her birth weight. In general, weight gain is assessed on a 4-week average: Birth to 3 months: a gain of 150–200 g a week; 3 to 6 months: a gain of 100–150 g a week; 6 to 12 months: a gain of 70–90 g a week.

  • Bowel actions: An infant’s first bowel actions consist of meconium, which is greenish-black. After 24–48 hours the meconium changes – first to brownish ‘transitional’ stools and then, by the third or fourth day, to typical breastfed infants’ stools, which are loose and mustard-yellow (sometimes with milk curds), Frequent, runny stools do not mean a breastfed infant has diarrhoea or lactose intolerance – they should simply be viewed as evidence of sufficient milk. The number of bowel motions of breastfed infants tends to decrease between 6 weeks and 3 months of age. Intervals of several days or more between stools are common. If the infant is receiving breast milk only and no other food or fluid, there is no cause for concern. Exclusively breastfed infants are rarely constipated.

Infants under the age of 6 months.

All mothers should be encouraged to breastfeed their babies exclusively and should be provided with information about why exclusive breastfeeding leads to the best outcomes for their baby.

Mothers should be enabled to achieve effective breastfeeding according to their needs including appropriate support with positioning and attachment, hand expression and understanding signs of effective feeding. Encourage mothers to use expressed breast milk when away from the infant and breastfeed when mother and infant are together. Inform parents about the wide variation in normal bowel function in infants who are breastfed.

Responsive feeding (previously referred to as demand feeding) is used to describe a feeding relationship which is sensitive, reciprocal and about more than nutrition. Mothers should be reassured that breastfeeding can be used to feed, comfort and calm babies or to ease maternal discomfort e.g. engorgement. Breastfed babies cannot be overfed or spoiled by too much feeding.

Offering supplementary feeds – water, glucose or infant formula – when there is no medical reason has been shown to adversely affect the establishment and maintenance of successful breastfeeding. If an infant is unable to take all feeds directly at the breast, expressed breast milk should be the preferred method of feeding. The use of supplementary feeds other than expressed breast milk is rarely necessary. Breastfeeding of preterm infants while in hospital is occasionally supplemented by formula feeding. Even if the mother does need to continue giving supplementary feeds indefinitely, she should be encouraged to continue breastfeeding before offering the supplementary feed. It should not be suggested that the infant be totally formula fed.

Exclusively breastfed infants do not require additional fluids up to 6 months of age.

Modified feeding regimes:

There are a number of clinical indications for a short term modified feeding regime in the early days after birth. Examples include preterm or small for gestational age babies and those who are excessively sleepy after birth. Some other babies require additional medical support including babies with jaundice, babies who are compromised at birth-infection /hypoxic, babies of diabetic mothers.

  • Partial breastfeeding: When exclusive breastfeeding is not possible, the value of continuing partial breastfeeding should be emphasized and mothers should be supported to maximize the amount of breast milk their baby receives. Mothers who give other feeds in conjunction with breastfeeding should be enabled to do so as safely as possible and with the least possible disruption to breastfeeding.

  • Formula Feeding: If an infant is not breastfed or is partially breastfed, commercial infant formulas should be used as an alternative to breast milk until 12 months of age. LBW infants, including those with VLBW, who cannot be fed mother's own milk or donor human milk should be fed standard infant formula until 6 months of age. VLBW infants who cannot be fed mother's own milk or donor human milk should be given preterm infant formula if they fail to gain weight despite adequate feeding with standard infant formula. Formula-fed infants may be given cooled boiled tap water, if additional fluids are needed.

Feeding sick or LBW babies in the neonatal period and after

  • LBW infants who are able to breastfeed should be put to the breast as soon as possible after birth when they are clinically stable. LBW infants should be exclusively breastfed until 6 months of age.

  • VLBW should be fed their mother’s own milk, preferably expressed breast milk. In babies weighing less than 1000g at birth, (<1000g), start enteral feeds at 10-15 ml/kg per day within 24hours of life, increase feeds by 15-20mls /kg/day, aim to reach full enteral feeding 150 -180 ml/kg per day by about 2 weeks. Administer 2 hourly feeds, with the remaining fluid requirement met by intravenous fluids. In babies weighing 1000g-1500g, aim to reach full enteral feeding 150-180 ml/kg per day by about one week (see the table below).

Ideally VLBW babies should be supplemented with :

  • vitamin D supplements at a dose ranging from 400 i.u to 1000 i.u. per day until 6 months of age.

  • daily calcium (120-140 mg/kg per day) and phosphorus (60-90 mg/kg per day) supplementation during the first months of life.

  • 2-4 mg/kg per day iron supplementation starting at 2 weeks.

Introducing solid food

Solid food should be started at around 6 months. At around this point, infants are physiologically and developmentally ready for new foods, and need more nutrients than can be provided by breast milk or formula. This is necessary to meet the infant’s increasing nutritional and developmental needs. By 12 months of age, a variety of nutritious foods from the five food groups is recommended.

Discourage mothers from using expensive, low nutrition supplements such as potato powder. Local paps – such as cuuroo (peanut / rice porridge), monoo (coos porridge) or corn/soya/bean mix are much cheaper and more nutritious.

It is not unusual for babies to refuse a new food the first time it is introduced. Mothers should try a food on at least 10 different occasions before they decide that a baby doesn’t like it.

Food and fluids to avoid:

  • Fruit juice is not necessary or recommended for infants under 12 months of age.

  • Tea, herbal teas and other drinks are of no known benefit to an infant and could possibly be harmful.

  • To prevent botulism, do not feed honey to infants aged under 12 months.

Key Issues for Nursing care

  • All health professionals who work with new mothers have the responsibility of raising awareness of the health benefits of breastfeeding and the potential risks of not breastfeeding. Encourage, support and promote exclusive breastfeeding to around 6 months of age and explain that infants do not require fluids other than breast milk for the first 6 months. Support the principles of the Baby Friendly Hospital Initiative.

  • Mothers who formula-feed should be enabled to do so as safely as possible through the offer of a demonstration and a discussion about how to prepare infant formula. Infant formula must be prepared according to instructions to the correct concentration. The importance of responsive feeding should be discussed with them and they should be encouraged to respond to cues that their baby is hungry as well as recognize their baby’s cues that they have had enough milk.

  • If mothers express and store breast milk, it is important that they follow correct procedures to ensure food safety and hygiene.

  • Encourage mothers to use expressed breast milk when away from the infant and breastfeed when mother and infant are together. Inform parents about the wide variation in normal bowel function in infants who are breastfed.

  • LBW infants who need to be fed by an alternative oral feeding method should be fed by cup and spoon.

  • Early post-natal period skin-to-skin contact between the mother and her newborn should be encouraged.
