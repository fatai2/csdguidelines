Fluid Management

Executive summary

Introduction

Fluid management is a critical aspect of patient care, especially in the inpatient medical setting. Each patient demands careful consideration of their individual fluid needs.

It is important to differentiate between maintenance and replacement fluids. Maintenance fluids should address the basic physiologic needs of the patient including both sensible (urination and defaecation) and insensible (sweat, respiratory) fluid losses. Replacement fluids address abnormal fluid losses such as vomiting, diarrhoea and losses through burns. The source of these losses will determine which fluids are required and how much.

Adequate fluid management depends on accurate estimation of input and output.

Target users

  • Doctors

  • Nurses

Target area of use

  • Outpatient department

  • Ward

Key areas of focus / New additions / Changes

This guideline addresses the ways to assess a patient’s volume status to determine their need for fluids in our setting.

Limitations

We do not have a wide range of different fluid types available.

Assessing fluid status

There are many ways to assess a patient’s volume status. These include clinical findings, changes in vital signs and laboratory markers.

Vital signs

  • Weight: Weight is a sensitive indicator of changes in volume status, but is difficult to assess accurately. Patients should be weighed at each clinic appointment and daily on the ward, where possible, using the same scales.

  • Heart rate: Tachycardia may indicate compensated hypovolaemic shock (although other causes include pain, fever, and anxiety).

  • Blood pressure: Falling blood pressure in the setting of tachycardia indicates decompensated hypovolaemia. Volume overload may be associated with either raised or low BP.

    • BP changes on standing may be more sensitive. A drop of at least 20 mm Hg systolic blood pressure or 10 mm Hg diastolic blood pressure within 2 to 5 minutes of quiet standing after 5 minutes of supine rest suggest significant dehydration (or failing baroreceptors in the elderly)

  • Respiratory rate: Increased respiratory rate may be an early finding in hypovolemic shock.

  • Urine output: Hypovolaemia may be indicated by a fall in output below 1.5 mL/kg per hour in children and greater than 1 mL/kg per hour in adults.

Physical examination findings

  • Fontanelle: Sunken fontanelle on the skull of an infant suggests hypovolaemia.

  • Tear production: Relevant in infants and children; Important to ask parents for their observations and evaluate the child while in the exam room.

  • Mucous membranes: Oral mucosa or tongue are dry, with sandpaper like texture in states of dehydration.

  • Tactile temperature of the skin: Classically find cool and clammy skin found in hypovolemic shock due to peripheral vasoconstriction causing hypoperfusion of skin especially at the extremities.

  • Skin turgor and eyeball appearances: Severe cases of dehydration might present with flaccid or tented skin. Eyeballs might also appear sunken into orbital cavities.

  • Jugular vein appearance: Distended jugular vein in volume overload state; Can also be found in patients with congestive heart failure who are euvolaemic but not pumping blood appropriately.

  • Capillary refill: Normally less than 3 seconds.

  • Oedema: Peripheral oedema can be a sign of volume overload or third spacing of intravascular fluid.

  • Peripheral pulses: Check brachial and femoral pulses in infants; Check radial or dorsalis pedis pulses in older patients. You may see fast and thready pulses in dehydration states.

Laboratory findings

  • Urea/creatinine: May indicate poor renal perfusion due to overload or hypovolaemia.

  • Transaminases: Elevation of AST or ALT due to hypoperfusion of hepatic tissue and subsequent tissue hypoxia causing hepatocyte injury.

  • Haemoconcentration: Can see elevated haematocrit due to a relative abundance of red blood cells relative to intravascular fluid volume

Calculating fluid needs

Fluid needs vary according to the patient’s size and underlying conditions. Paediatric and adults have different body compositions.

Weight based formulas are used to calculate maintenance fluid needs within a 24 hour period:

  • First 10 kg = 100 ml/kg per day

  • Next 10 to 20 kg = additional 50 mL/kg per day

  • Any remaining weight over 20 kg = additional 20 mL/kg per day

Elderly and obese patients may need less fluid than this formula suggests. Adults rarely need more than 3 litres per day for maintenance.

Fluid deficits or losses must be added to the total amount and the fluids distributed across a 24 hour period according to the urgency of need to replace losses.

Outputs that should be taken into account when calculating losses include:

  • Urine

  • Sweat

  • Respiration

  • Stool

  • Vomit

  • 3rd space losses

  • Haemorrhage

Calculating electrolyte needs

Maintenance fluids should usually provide:

  • 1 mmol/kg/day of potassium, sodium and chloride

  • 50-100 g/day of glucose (this is just enough to prevent starvation)

Giving fluids

Wherever possible, fluids should be given via the enteral route – orally or using an NG tube. Intravenous fluids should be preserved for patients who cannot tolerate enteral fluids or are shocked or have some other need for urgent fluid replacement.

A flow chart is supplied that helps with the application of the principles in this guideline to adult patients. The same principles apply to children, but the volumes are different.

Available fluids:

  • Enteral

    • Oral rehydration salts

    • ReSoMal (WHO rehydration fluid for malnourished children)

    • Infant formula

    • Expressed breast milk

    • Water

    • E-pap (corn-soya-bean mix)

  • Intravenous (iv)

    • Ringer’s lactate (sometimes called Hartmann’s)

    • Normal Saline (0.9% Sodium Chloride)

    • 5% Dextrose in water

    • 5% Dextrose in Normal saline

Fluids should be prescribed by doctors and administration should be documented by nursing staff. During administration, regular review of the patient’s vital signs, other examination findings and electrolytes and renal function are all essential.

Complications

  • Volume overload: recognised by rising HR, BP, RR, pulmonary oedema, hepatomegaly or peripheral oedema.

  • Electrolyte abnormalities including hyponatraemia, hypernatraemia, hypokalaemia etc: can only be recognised by monitoring blood results and should be managed according to the relevant clinical guidelines.

  • Metabolic acidosis: can result from use of normal saline – partly because it is slightly acidotic, but also because of the high chloride load. This can be avoided by the use of Ringer’s lactate.

Key issues for nursing care

  • Ensure inputs and outputs are recorded as accurately as possible.

  • Record patient’s observations (HR, BP, RR, weight) on a regular basis.

  • Check position of NG tubes before use as outlined in the relevant SOP.

  • Check cannulas are clean and in correct position before iv fluids are used.

  • Where available use syringe drivers or fluids infusers to control delivery of iv fluids to all patients, but especially to small children.
