Ascites

Executive summary

Introduction

Ascites is the build up of fluid in the peritoneal cavity. Ascites is a symptom or a sign and not a diagnosis. This guideline provides an approach to investigation, which should result in a diagnosis. Once the diagnosis is known, treatment options should become clearer.

Target users

  • Doctors

  • Nurses

Target area of use

  • Gate Clinic

  • OPD

  • Ward

Key areas of focus / New additions / Changes

Ascites is a symptom and not a diagnosis. New onset ascites requires rapid evaluation for its cause as an inpatient.

Limitations

We lack some of the diagnostic tests that could help us to reach a diagnosis and instead have to focus on symptom relief. We also lack albumin, meaning our paracentesis is limited to 5 litres per day in an adult.

Presenting symptoms and signs

History

  • Abdominal pain

  • Abdominal swelling

  • Reduced appetite

  • Shortness of breath – worse on lying flat

  • If infected (Bacterial Peritonitis) – fever, tenderness and confusion

  • A history should be taken to cover the potential causes – liver disease, heart failure, renal failure, abdominal infection, abdominal cancers and malnutrition – there are guidelines on several of the subjects to guide things.

Examination findings

To be guided by potential aetiology from the history, but thorough abdominal examination is necessary looking for signs of liver failure.

It is important to distinguish ascites from one of the other causes of abdominal distention (the Five F’s being Fluid (Ascites), Foetus, Fat, Flatus, Faeces).

Investigations

All new cases of ascites should be admitted to the ward as an emergency for evaluation.

Spontaneous Bacterial Peritonitis (SBP) has a high mortality and must be excluded in all as soon as possible.

All patients should have:

  • FBC, U&Es, LFTs, bone profile, consider TSH (hypothyroid is a rare cause of ascites), INR, HIV

  • Chest X-ray

  • Urine dip

  • USS abdomen

  • Ascitic tap – sent for LDH, protein and glucose from, MC&S and AFBs.

Consider:

  • TSH (hypothyroid is a rare cause of ascites)

  • INR

  • HIV

If the suspicion for TB is high then it is worth repeating the ascitic tap and sending a GeneXpert. It may also be worth considering an induced sputum.

Interpreting the ascitic tap

Cloudy ascitic fluid should be treated as SBP until proven otherwise (assuming you have excluded the possibility of an acute abdomen with perforated viscus).

Bloody ascitic fluid may indicate cancer.

Cell count and Gram stain

WCC of over 500 or neutrophil count of over 250 cells in the ascites should be treated as SBP.

A raised lymphocyte count could indicate TB. It very unusual to grow an organism in the ascites and a poor prognostic sign to do so. Typical organisms are gram negative bacteria. Patients on prophylactic antibiotics against SBP have an increased chance of a gram positive organism being the causative agent.gut translocation

Serum to ascites albumin gradient (SAAG)

This is the total protein minus the ascites protein in g/L. This offers a good guide to further investigations and making a diagnosis of the underlying disease. Glucose

Low glucose could indicate the presence of infection and should be correlated with cell count.

LDH

Ascitic fluid protein/serum protein > 0.5, ascitic fluid LDH/serum LDH > 0.6, ascitic fluid LDH > 400. Presence of any 2 of these three findings is usually associated with TB, malignancy or pancreatitis. Absence of all three usually indicates hepatic cause.

AFB

The sensitivity of AFB smear for TB in the ascites is around 2% and a negative test should therefore not stop treatment if the diagnosis seems otherwise likely. GeneXpert is not optimised for ascites, but has higher sensitivity and should generally be preferred for this indication.

Treatment

Definitive treatment relies upon treating the underlying cause. The diagnosis therefore determines the treatment plan. However, symptom relief can be provided in one of two main ways. These two methods may be combined to produce symptomatic relief.

Paracentesis – In the absence of albumin, only up to 5 litres can be drained in one attempt. This should be done in a haemodynamically stable patient, so treating SBP for 48 hours before drainage is a good idea. It can be done for symptom relief in the setting of TB (though this is based on case reports) but should be avoided if possible as the TB therapy will result in the ascites gradually resolving anyway.

Diuretics – In all conditions apart from cirrhosis, a loop diuretic is the mainstay of therapy with other diuretics added for effect when weight loss is slow or becomes static. In cirrhosis an aldosterone antagonist, such as spironolactone, is first line with a loop diuretic added if necessary.

Give spironolactone 100 mg OD. If this is inadequate, add furosemide 40 mg OD. Increase the doses step wise, each time increasing the spironolactone before the furosemide, up to maximum doses of spironolactone 400 mg per day and furosemide 160 mg per day.

Key Issues for Nursing care

  • Measurement of weight on a daily basis.

  • Accurate fluid balance during ascitic drainage.
