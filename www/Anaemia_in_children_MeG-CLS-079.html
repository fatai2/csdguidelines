Anaemia in children

Executive summary

Introduction 

Anaemia in childhood is defined as a haemoglobin (Hb) concentration below established cut-off levels for age, sex and race and altitude levels. WHO has defined these cut-off as:

  • <11 g/dL in children aged 6-59 months,

  • <11.5 g/dL in children aged 5-11 years

  • and < 12 g/dL in older children (aged 12-14).

The likely cause of childhood anaemia varies depending on the area of the world that the child lives in. Overall, iron deficiency (usually because of diet) is the most common cause but, in the developing world, infectious diseases such as malaria, helminthic infections, HIV and tuberculosis are also important.

Anaemia is not a diagnosis but a sign of a disease process.

Red blood cells are made in the bone marrow by erythropoiesis, then released into the circulation as reticulocytes, developing into mature red blood cells 1 day later. These mature cells circulate for around 3 months and are then removed by macrophages. In a healthy person, the rate of blood cell production and the rate of loss is balanced; in a patient with anaemia this balance is disrupted, for example due to reduced production or increased destruction of red blood cells.

Causes of anaemia

The causes of anaemia can be acquired or inherited. Where possible the cause should be identified as different types of anaemia need different treatment. If the cause cannot be identified, there should still be an attempt to classify which group the cause is likely to fall into.

  1. Malaria

  2. Iron deficiency anaemia (Microcytic hypochromic) is the most common type of anaemia worldwide.

Causes include:

  • reduced intake of iron examples of foods rich in iron include meat, eggs, leafy green vegetables and iron fortified foods;

  • reduced absorption of iron - such as occurs in coeliac disease;

  • increased iron requirements as in growth spurts and chronic haemolysis;

  • blood loss – gastrointestinal diseases like peptic ulcer and hookworm infestation, menstruation, haematuria and drugs e.g. NSAID;

  • beta-Thalassaemia minor/ trait – usually asymptomatic, but may be associated with microcytic red cells;

  • sickle cell anaemia;

  • rarer causes include anaemia of chronic disease, lead poisoning and sideroblastic anaemia.

  1. Normocytic anaemia – anaemia with normal sized red blood cells seen in

  • anaemia of chronic disease seen in chronic kidney disease, rheumatological diseases;

  • combined deficiencies – iron and folate deficiencies in coeliac disease;

  • haematological malignancies – acute or chronic leukaemia;

  • acute blood loss – haemorrhage;

  • organ failure – kidney, liver, thyroid and pituitary.

  1. Macrocytic anaemia – the red blood cells are large but have low haemoglobin concentration. Often associated with failure to thrive. Causes include:

  • Vit B12 deficiency due to:

    • reduced intake, seen in vegetarians and folate deficiency in elderly & alcoholics

    • reduced absorption in pernicious anaemia, post gastrectomy, post-ileal resection in Crohn’s disease, stagnant loops, fish tapeworm infestation

  • Folate deficiency due to:

    • increased requirements – folate deficiency seen in pregnancy, chronic haemolysis, dialysis and exfoliative dermatitis

    • reduced absorption in coeliac disease, tropical sprue, Crohn’s disease and stagnant loops

    • drugs which affect absorption: phenytoin, sodium valproate, combined oral contraceptive

    • drugs which oppose folate: methotrexate, septrin

  • Drugs which directly cause megaloblastic anaemia: alcohol; hydroxycarbamide (hydroxyurea), azathioprine, azidothymidine (AZT), cytosine – arabinoside


  1. Haemolytic anaemia: where red blood cells are destroyed faster than they can be made, due to:

  • inherited conditions such as sickle cell disease or thalassaemia;

  • stressors such as infections, drugs, snake and spider venom, and certain foods;

  • autoimmune disease such as haemolytic disease of the newborn;

  • direct damage to red blood cells by vascular grafts, prosthetic heart values; tumours; severe burns; exposure to certain chemicals.

  1. Aplastic anaemia: occurs when the bone fails producing enough new blood cells. Causes include: idiopathic causes, severe nutritional deficiencies such as vitamin B12 or folate, infections causes such as hepatitis, viruses, Epstein – Barr (EBV) virus, HIV, parvovirus and mycobacteria; exposure to radiation.

Grading of anaemia

Anaemia can be graded according to the haemoglobin.

Grade Haemoglobin
1 mild 9.6-13
2 moderate 8.1-9.5
3 severe 5.1-8.0
4 life threatening ≤ 5

Presenting symptoms and signs

Relevant background history

  • Ethnic origin.

  • Evidence of blood loss – haemoptysis, melaena, haematuria, menorrhagia.

  • Diet.

  • Chronic infections – infections such as infective endocarditis and osteomyelitis can lead to anaemia of chronic disease.

  • Drug history – certain drugs can trigger haemolysis in G6PD deficiency.

  • Family history – for inherited anaemias.

  • Child's sex – eg haemolytic anaemias are more likely in girls; G6PD deficiency is more common in boys.

  • Past medical history/neonatal history.

  • Recent travel.

Symptoms

May be asymptomatic, but common symptoms include fatigue, shortness of breath, lethargy, poor concentration, listlessness, failure to thrive / poor growth. Also:

  • Left upper quadrant pain (if there is associated splenomegaly).

  • Right upper quadrant pain (secondary to cholelithiasis in haemolytic anaemia).

  • Symptoms related to underlying disease pathology - eg acute pain in sickle cell crises and chronic / recurrent diarrhoea, which can suggest a malabsorption syndrome such as coeliac disease.

Management at the Gate Clinic

Mild anaemias with Hb of 10-10.9 g/dl or PCV 30-32.7% can be managed at the Gate Clinic.

Take a brief history with relevant past medical history and examination; send the patient to the lab and look for malaria parasites and the PCV or Hb.

Treat malaria appropriately if present.

Deworm with mebendazole 100 mg BD for 3 days (for children aged over 1 year) or a single dose of albendazole 200 mg for children aged 1-2 years or 400 mg for those aged older than 2 years of age.

Give 1 month of ferrous sulphate 15 mg/kg per day or ferrous fumarate 9-10 mg/kg per day.

Refer patients to the OPD if

  • Hb < 10 g/dl,

  • active blood loss,

  • the patient is very sick,

  • history of recurrent blood transfusions

  • any concerning history or examination findings

  • the patient returns to the clinic.

Management at the OPD

Examination findings

  • Pallor, pale conjunctiva examine conjunctivae, nail beds, palmar creases.

  • Jaundice, scleral icterus Jaundice – this may be present in haemolytic anaemias.

  • Petechiae and bruising – may be a sign of thrombocytopenia secondary to marrow aplasia or malignancy or of vasculitis.

  • Dysmorphic features of Fanconi's anaemia – small stature, small head, frontal bossing, absent thumbs, hyperpigmented skin.

  • Plot weight, height and head circumference – in chronic anaemia, growth can be affected, usually with preservation of head circumference.

  • Cardiovascular system – look for exertional tachycardia, a systolic flow murmur. Gallop rhythm, cardiomegaly and hepatomegaly are signs of congestive cardiac failure.

  • Splenomegaly – this occurs in chronic haemolysis and malignancy.

  • Papulovesicular lesions on the feet – this may indicate hookworm infestation.


Red flags (consider admission)

  • Hb <6g/dl

  • Tachycardia, cardiac murmur or signs of heart failure

  • Features of haemolysis: dark urine, jaundice

  • Associated reticulocytopenia

  • Presence of nucleated red blood cells on blood film

  • Associated thrombocytopenia or neutropenia may indicate malignancy or an infiltrative disorder

  • Severe vitamin B12 or folate deficiency

  • Need for transfusion

Investigations

At first consultation request a FBC and blood film comment to classify the kind of anaemia. Malaria blood film is also important during the transmission season.

  • Direct Coombs test, reticulocyte count, Hb genotype, ESR (but beware that this will be raised by severe anaemia itself).

  • Biochemistry – electrolytes, urea and creatinine, liver function tests, bone profile.

  • Radiology to look for evidence of myeloma, metastases or bony malignancy.

  • Ultrasound studies to look for other malignancies.

  • Gastrointestinal investigations including faecal occult blood, upper and lower gastrointestinal endoscopy and barium studies of the bowel.

Other tests that would be useful, but are usually not available to us include:

  • Serum iron ferritin and total iron binding capacity

  • Serum B12 and folate.

  • Bone marrow aspirate or biopsy studies (this is sometimes available at EFSTH).

Treatment

Moderate anaemia in haemodynamically stable patients can be treated in the OPD. Patient with the following problems should be admitted to the ward (Fajara) or referred emergently to Bwiam (Keneba):

  • Severe to life threatening anaemia.

  • Signs of heart failure, tachycardia, tachypnoea and hepatomegaly.

  • Severe malaria with anaemia.

  • Acute massive blood loss.

  • Extensive severe burns.

The treatment of anaemia depends on the suspected cause:

Iron deficiency anaemia: Treat with oral iron supplementation (3 mg/kg per day of elemental iron, which is equivalent to 15 mg/kg per day of ferrous sulphate or 9 mg/kg per day of ferrous fumarate.)

The efficiency of iron absorption is reduced as the dose increases. Where the Hb is < 8 g/dl, increasing frequency will increased absorption somewhat, but the side effects may become more prominent. Greater absorption is achieved when you prescribe the iron TDS on alternate days rather than giving it BD every day.

A rise in haemoglobin >1 g/dL after 4 weeks supports the diagnosis of iron deficiency, and supplementation should continue for 2 additional months to replenish iron stores.

Normocytic anaemia: The most common normocytic anaemias are those found in association with chronic infections such as inflammatory diseases and malignant diseases. The presence of this blood picture may indicate a serious underlying disease, hence the main priority is to treat any identifiable underlying cause and stop any precipitating drugs.

However, normocytic anaemia may reflect mixed iron and folate deficiency, so if no other cause is identified, a trial of treatment is indicated.

Macrocytic anaemia: The most common cause of megaloblastic anaemia in children is lack of folic acid or vitamin B12. Correct any deficiency and treat any underlying cause.

Vitamin B12 deficiency – give im cyanocobalamin 1 mg alternate days until 10 doses have been given, then monthly until the full blood count is normal. Continue treatment every three months, unless a clear cause for the deficiency was identified.

Folate deficiency: give folic acid 5 mg orally once daily for two to three months, start folate supplements after vitamin B12 deficiency has been treated to prevent neurological complications.

Haemolytic anaemia: A child with haemolytic anaemia is often treated by a haematologist. The treatment will vary depending on the cause and how severe the anaemia is. Treatment may include blood transfusion, corticosteroids, and intravenous immunoglobulin.

Children with warm autoimmune haemolytic anaemia may be treated with oral prednisolone 1 mg/kg per day for 3-4 weeks, if there is a clinical response then taper slowly over 6 months and stop. If there is no response after 3 weeks, consider another diagnosis. If there is a poor response after tapering over 6 months, consider as prednisolone dependent, and continue at the minimum effective dose 0.1-0.2 mg/kg per day or consider second line treatment.

Aplastic anaemia: Treatment for aplastic anaemia depends on the cause. This diagnosis requires further investigation to identify underlying cause. For mild aplastic anaemia treatment may not be needed. Treatment may include blood transfusions, platelet transfusions, antibiotics. If possible refer to a haematologist.

Management on the ward

Take a proper and detailed history.

Do a complete physical examination – general and systemic.

Arrange investigations as for outpatients.

Treatment on the ward

  • Treat malaria if present

  • Treat any infections

  • Treat the underlying cause of the anaemia.

  • Consider transfusion in severe or life-threatening anaemia.

Blood transfusion is only required if the child is compromised and on the verge of high-output cardiac failure.

Indications for blood transfusion:

  • All children with Hb ≤ 4g/dl or haematocrit <12%

  • Less severely anaemic children with Hb 4 - 6g/dl with any of the following clinical features:

    • Shock

    • Impaired consciousness

    • Heart failure

    • Very high malaria parasitaemia

Administration of blood

Obtain informed consent for blood transfusion.

Group and cross match blood.

Write a transfusion order (to include type of blood product to be administered, volume or amount to be administered, rate of infusion or duration, date and time of administration, special transfusion requirements if any, pre intra or post medication related to the transfusion if required, including dose, route and time to administer (eg pre-medication with diphenhydramine if documented history of previous transfusion reactions, or intra or post medication such as IV furosemide may be considered if patient is at increased risk for fluid overload complications).

Transfuse with fresh whole blood 20 mls/kg over 3-4 hours (or packed cells 10 mls/kg if available).

Give IV furosemide 1 mg/kg stat post transfusion if the blood is given for a cause of anaemia not associated with a reduction in blood volume.

Monitor pre- intra- and post -transfusion vital signs.

Complications of anaemia:

Prognosis of anaemia is good in most cases. However, complications may arise in some cases due to multiple organ dysfunctions.

Possible complications include:

  • Congestive Cardiac Failure (CCF)

  • High risk of infection

  • Attention-deﬁcit disorders

  • Developmental disorders

  • Intellectual Disability (ID)

  • Picca syndrome

  • Plummer-Vinson syndrome (a triad of iron-deﬁciency anaemia, glossitis, and oesophageal webs).

Key Issues for Nursing care

  • Nutritional counselling and family support to prevent further iron deficiency anaemia

  • To improve on personal hygiene and avoid walking on bare feet.

  • Counselling on hereditary disease conditions.

  • Advice on compliance to iron supplementation

  • Adherence to transfusion order on the ward.

  • Advice on disease control and prevention
