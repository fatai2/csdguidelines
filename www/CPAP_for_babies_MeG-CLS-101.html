Continuous Positive Airway Pressure (CPAP) for babies

Executive summary

Introduction 

Clinical Service Department has one Diamedica Baby CPAP machine. Please also refer to the manufacturers’ instruction manual (in guidelines folder on Sharepoint) for a full guide of how to set the machine up and maintain it. Diamedica’s Baby CPAP generates its own oxygen and medical air which can be delivered at a variable concentrations.

CPAP is a form of non-invasive ventilation for patients who are breathing spontaneously. A continuous oxygen and air mix is delivered under pressure to the patient via nasal prongs. Nasal CPAP can help maintain clear airways, improve gas exchange, improve lung volume and reduce the work of breathing.

Indications, complications and how to use CPAP for babies.

Limitations

This CPAP machine can be used for babies up to approximately 1 year old who weigh up to 10kg.

Indications and Contraindications

Indications:

  • Reversible airway or neuromuscular disease

    • Deteriorating patient

    • Significantly increased work of breathing despite simple measures

    • Apnoeas and bradycardias (particularly in babies with bronchiolitis)

    • Increasing oxygen requirements

    • Atelectasis on chest X-ray

Contraindications:

  • Respiratory arrest

  • Un-cooperative patient

  • Unable to protect own airway or upper airway obstruction

  • Oral/facial abnormality or injury meaning a good seal cannot be achieved

  • Known pneumothorax

  • Patients with underlying condition in whom CPAP is unlikely to make an improvement

Considerations

Before starting CPAP, the baby should be fully assessed by the doctor with baseline blood tests and a chest x-ray. An orogastric tube should be fitted for feeds and to aspirate air from the abdomen if required. The consultant on call should be made aware of any patients being started on CPAP.

Fixation Technique

  • Use the Diamedica manual in the guidelines folder on Sharepoint to set up the CPAP machine

  • There are official Diamedica Baby CPAP videos on youtube which are very useful – please watch them (https://youtu.be/jXTvmI_Rrrg)

  • Initial settings should be with the oxygen flow meter at 5 litres/minute and the air flow meter at 5 litres/minute in order to deliver a total flow of 10 litres/minute. The initial pressure should be set at 6 cm H20.

  • Final Check:

    • Bonnet/head gear well down over the infant’s head;

    • Ears well covered and flat against head;

    • Prongs/mask correctly positioned;

    • Nose in normal position not squashed or pulled upwards;

    • Eyes clearly visible;

    • Circuit is stable and secure;

    • Infant receiving required level of nasal CPAP.

Monitoring and Adjustments

The CPAP can be adjusted to provide pressures of up to 10cm H20 as well as oxygen concentrations ranging from 28-95%.

Settings should be adjusted according to the observations of the baby as well as the clinical assessment.

The pressure can be altered using the dial on the bottle at the side of the machine.

The oxygen concentration can be varied by increasing and decreasing the proportion or air and oxygen flow but beware that the total flow should remain at least 9l/min to maintain the pressure. If the circuit is not bubbling then you should increase the total flow as it may require slightly more than 9l/min in some circumstances.

Feeding

Feeding and adequate hydration while babies are on CPAP is vital. Ideally babies should receive orogastric feeds 2 hourly to minimize their work of breathing but IV fluids can also be considered. It is possible to breastfeed with CPAP in place, which may be required to soothe and settle some babies so that the CPAP is better tolerated.

Complications

Clinical:

  • Inadequate ventilation

  • Overventilation

  • Pneumothorax

  • Decreased cardiac output due to increased intrathoracic pressure

  • Gastric distension

  • Mucus plugging/build-up of secretions

  • Oral/nasal/eye irritation

  • Pressure areas from tubing or straps

  • Aspiration

Mechanical:

  • Mechanical failure of ventilation delivery due to flow rate being too low (air flow + oxygen flow should be at least 9-10 litres/minute to generate bubbles in the water bottle

  • Mechanical failure of humidification

  • Interface damage, leak, dislodgement or misfit

  • Circuit leak and damage

Troubleshooting

  • Assess patient for adequacy of airway, breathing and ventilation, consider gentle oral or nasal suctioning

  • Consider comfort measures such as swaddling, breastfeeding and paracetamol if infant appears to ‘dislike’ the CPAP. Consider sedation as a last resort.

  • Check interface, circuit and device, remove any condensation from tubing.

  • Contact on-call doctor.

Discontinuing Nasal CPAP

Discontinuation of CPAP should be considered when the general condition of the baby is stable, with oxygen requirements less than 40% and minimal CPAP requirements. The baby should remain closely monitored for the following 24 hours in case of deterioration.

Key Issues for Nursing care

  • Patients on CPAP should be closely monitored with 2 hourly observations as a minimum with checks of circuit, humidification, temperature delivered and baby (position of prongs and straps).

  • Every 4 hours the nasal prongs should be removed, checked for secretions and gentle nasal massage applied to the infant.

  • Mouth care should be carried out every 4 hours

  • If the baby is not receiving any nutrition then 4-6 hourly blood glucose should be recorded

  • Strict fluid balance chart must be maintained and the doctor called if the urine output drops below 2 ml/kg/hour

  • Circuits should be cleaned weekly (see the manufacturers’ manual for instructions)

  • Nasal prongs should be cleaned daily

  • Refresh water daily with sterile water

  • All equipment will be decontaminated between each patient and any single use items should be disposed of.
